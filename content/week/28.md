## Unit Nº 4: Extra Credit

We know some folks were surprised by (and unhappy with) their [fourth project evaluations](/week/23/#project-4-recap). We stand by these—but do want to give another opportunity to incorporate our feedback and also demonstrate your understanding of the unit (and course) material.

Traditionally, [we’ve allowed](https://typography-interaction-2425.github.io/week/28/#:~:text=You%20can%20re%2Dsubmit%20Project%204%20for%20re%2Devaluation!) people to revise and resubmit these projects and better their grades. But in light of the early and ubiquitous presence of “AI” in these—where the LLM&NoBreak;s were in control—we don’t think this would really be useful here. We need to see what *you* can do.

### So we’re offering an alternate extra-credit assignment:

- Choose two or more artworks—preferably on a theme or in conversation—that you can find online. You’ll be *interpreting* these in our medium of the web.

- Make sure it is something where you can credit the original artist, and link to more information about the work.

- These are probably more approachable if they are *non-representational*—so not photos or paintings. Think posters, abstract art, minimalism, geometric forms.

	[These](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/189312) [sorts](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/122104) [of](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/65882) [things](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/79816) [would](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/7734) [work](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/80528) [well](https://www.moma.org/collection/works/93425)! But up to you. <!-- .note -->

- In a new repo, using only HTML elements and CSS properties, you will “draw” each of your artworks. You can also make use of text—but no images! Think of HTML elements as your “daubs of paint.” If you need to make a mark, make an element.

	We are not looking for [Diana Smith](https://diana-adrianne.com) level of fidelity—but are expecting *interpretation*. Sometimes [approximations](https://x.com/dz/status/991365966553591808) are [better](https://x.com/mdotslash/status/991510235989721088)! <!-- .note -->

- Since these are primarily visual elements, non-semantic containers (like `div`) would be appropriate. But use semantic tags (like [`figure`/`figcaption`](/topic/images/#figure-figcaption)&#x202F;) outside, where it makes sense.

- Include a [*tombstone*](https://harvardartmuseums.org/article/writing-on-the-wall) for each artwork—the artist, title, and date. And provide a link to the original source for your artwork!

- Avail yourself of [advanced styles](/topic/advanced-css/), but still demonstrate thorough use of [nesting](/topic/css/#oh-also-nesting), [`grid`](/topic/grid/), [variables](/topic/responsive/#briefly-css-variables), and overall *structured*, *systematic* thinking. No (or at least *very* few) “[magic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magic_number_(programming))!”

- These should be [responsive](/topic/responsive/#viewport-meta-tag), of course. And mind your [metadata](/topic/link-meta/)!

- We also want you to include some basic-but-interesting *interactivity* [via Javascript](/topic/javascript/), by toggling between states. Think: *before* and *after*.

- Think of how the works can be enlivened, here! Make it “webby.”

- These will be due at the end of the semester, along with your [*Index* project](/project/index/), on May 15. Be sure to add a link on those, as well.

- We’ll evaluate these based on your creativity, engagement, and demonstration of *your own* understanding of our medium, the web.

- This will be applied towards improving your Project 4 grade.
