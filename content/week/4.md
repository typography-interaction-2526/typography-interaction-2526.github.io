## Some Things to Discuss

After going through your [Figma sketches](https://www.figma.com/files/project/274156991) (everyone should have feedback there now), we noticed a few trends across the board:
<!-- .balance -->

1. **There weren’t a lot of *distinct* sketch directions.**

	Many folks’ work landed much closer to “explorations on one theme” rather than “three distinct directions.” (A font/color change alone does not always make new direction.)

	In addition to not satisfying our [expectations for the assignment](/project/1#sketches), this can also only limit your horizons for the project. Sketching is meant to initially *broaden* these, from which we can later narrow. But you can’t refine (and we can’t respond to) what we don’t see. We want to see more varied exploration, here.

1. **It also wasn’t very… *sketchy*.**

	Lots of “here are my only three frames.” We want to see more *in-progress* and more experimentation. These don’t have to be complete thoughts. Sketches are *not* presentations. Be looser.

1. **Lots of *too-small* type.**

	We did say that to some extent, scale/zoom doesn’t <nobr>matter—</nobr>it is about the relationships. But we doubt everyone was drawing at 200% zoom, so the type was often too small. Put yourself in the mind/eyes of your reader! It’ll depend on the typeface, but ~`18`–`24px` is probably a safe starting point.

1. **Multi-column, *print* layouts.**

	We left lots of notes for folks about their multi-column layouts. Columns are a powerful, structural design tool—but are complicated in digital, where the user only ever sees a portion of your page while scrolling. We can’t expect people to scroll down one column, then zigzag back up another.

	Keep in mind that your user never sees “the whole canvas” as we do in Figma—this is a weak point of the tool. Always think about how your user will flow (scroll) through your page—especially when the primary purpose is reading, as with this project.

	Relatedly, nobody worked “frameless!” Remember that with all of our tools, their form and limitations can become our own.

1. **In the [*Semantic DOM*](/project/1/#semantic-dom) we’ve seen so far, not enough *structure*.**

	Lots of text fragments (no `<p>` or `<h#>`), very flat hierarchy, little overall organization. You will need more groupings/containers *around* your text elements to work towards your designs. We want to see a lot more [semantic structuring](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Glossary/Semantics#semantic_elements) of your document. Boxes in boxes!

1. **LLMs.**

	We suspect several of you used LLMs to “write” your reading responses. You know who you are. That’s really discouraging when we’ve asked for *your thoughts.* It’s [cheating yourself](/syllabus/#llm-s-and-artificial-intelligence); it’s unfair to your peers (who do the work); ultimately, it’s a waste of all our time.

1. Okay, for real, let’s switch gears to CSS. Create a `style.css` in the same folder as your `index.html`.

	Recall that CSS [can live](/topic/css/#where-css-lives) also live in a `<style>` tag, or even inline on an element itself—but these don’t scale very well. So we’ll be using an *external* (not in the HTML) stylesheet.

1. In the `<head>` of your HTML file, link to your `style.css`:

	```html
	<!doctype html>
	<html>
		<head>
			<title>Your page title</title>
			<link href="style.css" rel="stylesheet">
		</head>
		<body>
			<!-- Your HTML content. -->
		</body>
	</html>
	```

1. I always like to add something obvious in, to make sure it is all connected. Add this to your `style.css`:

	```css
	body { color: tomato; }
	```

	If you hop back to your page in the browser, you should see the color change! If not, your HTML isn’t seeing your CSS. Retrace your steps.

1. Let’s also try adding in [a reset](/topic/css/#resets) to start with a *clean slate*:

	```html <!-- style="max-inline-size: calc(var(--layout--page) - var(--typography--line--2))" -->
	<link href="https://typography-interaction-2425.github.io/assets/reset.css" rel="stylesheet">
	```

	Try [commenting](/topic/html/#comments) <kbd>⌘&thinsp;~&thinsp;Ctrl</kbd> <kbd>/</kbd> this line in and out, then looking again in the browser! Note the differences, as the *user-agent* styles are reset.

1. We’ll bring this file in locally, to avoid [a *dependency*](https://research.swtch.com/deps#what_is_a_dependency).

	Create a `reset.css` in your repo, as we did with `style.css`. You can go to [the course reset](https://typography-interaction-2425.github.io/assets/reset.css) in your browser, and copy the code into your local file. Then update the `link` in your `head`:

	```html
	<link href="reset.css" rel="stylesheet">
	```

1. Finally, let’s change our type!

	Go to [Google Fonts](https://fonts.google.com/) and find what you need. On the [*specimen* page](https://fonts.google.com/specimen/EB+Garamond) (the page for a font family), click the <samp>Get font</samp> button—this will add them to a sort of “shopping bag” up top.

	We’ll start by linking to it. Click <samp><> Get embed code</samp> to see our options, choose `<link>`. Select your weights, if necessary (only bring in what you will use), and copy the code there into your `<head>`:

	```html <!-- style="max-inline-size: calc(var(--layout--page) - var(--typography--line--3))" -->
	<link href="https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=EB+Garamond:ital,wght@0,400..800;1,400..800&display=swap" rel="stylesheet">;
	```

	And then we’ll apply it—in this case, to everything, as `font-family` is [*inherited*](/topic/css/#and-inheritance): <!-- .add-before -->

	```css
	body { font-family: 'EB Garamond', serif }
	```

	You can also `@import` right into the top of your stylesheet, if you prefer: <!-- .add-before--2 .secondary -->

	```css <!-- style="max-inline-size: calc(var(--layout--page) - var(--typography--line--3))" -->
	@import url('https://fonts.googleapis.com/css2?family=EB+Garamond:ital,wght@0,400..800;1,400..800&display=swap');
	```
